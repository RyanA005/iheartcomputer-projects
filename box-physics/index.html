what is a physics engine?

a physics engine is a software component that simulates physical systems, such as rigid body dynamics, fluid dynamics, and collision detection.

they are used in a wide range of applications, including video games, virtual reality, robotics, and scientific simulations.



physics engines allow developers to create realistic and interactive environments without having to manually code the complex physics interactions.





why are physics engines important today?

physics engines are crucial for creating immersive and realistic experiences in video games and virtual reality.

they enable developers to simulate complex interactions between objects, such as collisions, gravity, and fluid dynamics, which enhance the overall user experience.

in scientific simulations, physics engines allow researchers to model and study physical phenomena that would be difficult or impossible to replicate in real life.

in robotics, physics engines are used to simulate the behavior of robots in various environments, helping engineers design and test their creations before building physical prototypes.





core components of a physics engine

1. Rigid Body Dynamics: simulates the motion and interaction of solid objects.

2. Collision Detection: determines when and where objects collide.

3. Constraint Solvers: handle constraints between objects, such as joints or ropes.

4. Fluid Dynamics: simulates the behavior of fluids, such as water or smoke.

5. Soft Body Dynamics: simulates deformable objects, such as cloth or jelly.

very basic architecture of a physics engine


1. Initialization: set up the simulation environment, load objects, and initialize their properties.

2. Simulation Loop: repeatedly update the simulation by calculating forces, updating object states, and handling collisions.

3. Rendering: visualize the simulation results, often in real-time for games or interactive applications.






rigid body dynamics

rigid body dynamics simulates the motion and interaction of solid objects that do not deform.

it is based on Newton's laws of motion, which describe how objects move in response to forces.

the core equation for rigid body dynamics are:

F = m * a (force equals mass times acceleration)

τ = I * α (torque equals moment of inertia times angular acceleration)

to simulate rigid body dynamics, we need to calculate the forces and acting on each object, and then update their positions and orientations based on the equations of motion.





rigid body representation

to represent a rigid body in a simple physics engine, we will use the following data structure that includes the following properties:

1. mass - the mass of the object, which determines how it responds to forces.

2. inverse mass - commonly cache 1/mass to prevent division ops

3. position - the location of the object in 3D space.

4. velocity - the rate of change of the object's position.

5. forces - the external influences acting on the object, which can change its motion.

6. bounding shape - the collision information used in broad phase.

7. collision shape - the collision information used in narrow phase.


production engines use more fields to represent complicated motion:

1. orientation - the rotation of the object in 3D space, often represented as a quaternion or Euler angles.

2. angular velocity - the rate of change of the object's orientation.

3. torques - the rotational forces acting on the object, which can change its orientation.

4. inertia tensor - a measure of an object's resistance to changes in its rotation, which depends on its mass distribution.





numerical integration

to update the positions and orientations of objects based on the forces, we need to use numerical integration methods.

a(t) = F(t) / m

the simplest method is the Euler method:

v(t + Δt) = v(t) + a(t) * Δt

x(t + Δt) = x(t) + v(t) * Δt

however, the Euler method can be unstable and inaccurate for large time steps, so most custom engines use Semi Implicit Euler:

v(t + Δt) = v(t) + a(t) * Δt

x(t + Δt) = x(t) + v(t + Δt) * Δt

this method is more stable and allows for larger time steps, but it can still introduce errors over time, so more advanced methods like Runge-Kutta or Verlet integration are often used in production engines.





collision detection

collision detection is the process of determining when and where objects in the simulation collide.

it is a crucial component of a physics engine, as it allows for realistic interactions between objects, such as bouncing, sliding, and stacking.

collision detection typically involves two main steps:

1. Broad Phase: quickly identify pairs of objects that may be colliding using simple bounding volumes (e.g., AABBs or spheres).

2. Narrow Phase: perform detailed collision checks on the identified pairs using more complex shapes (e.g., polygons or meshes) to determine if they are actually colliding and to calculate a contact manifold.





broad phase collision detection

the broad phase is the first step in collision detection, where we quickly identify pairs of objects that may be colliding using simple bounding volumes.

we are asking: could the objects possibly be colliding?
common broad phase algorithms include:

1. Spatial Partitioning: dividing the simulation space into a grid or tree structure (e.g., quadtree, octree) to efficiently query for nearby objects.


2. Sweep and Prune: sorting objects along an axis and checking for overlapping intervals to identify potential collisions.


3. Bounding Volume Hierarchies (BVH): organizing objects into a hierarchy of bounding volumes to quickly eliminate non-colliding pairs.


typically, objects are placed in a "bounding volume" which is most commonly an AABB. This bounding volume is used during broad-phase for quicker calculations.





narrow phase collision detection

the narrow phase is the second step in collision detection, where we perform detailed collision checks on the identified pairs of objects from the broad phase.

this involves checking the actual shapes of the objects (e.g., polygons, meshes) to determine if they are colliding and to calculate a contact manifold.

common narrow phase algorithms include:

1. Separating Axis Theorem (SAT) - checks if there is a separating axis between two convex shapes

2. Gilbert-Johnson-Keerthi (GJK) Algorithm & Expanding Polytope Algorithm (EPA) - algorithm pair for convex shapes
3. Bounding Volume Hierarchies (BVH) Narrow Phase - uses the BVH from the broad phase to perform more detailed checks on the actual geometry of the objects.

4. Triangle Mesh Collision Detection - checks for collisions between complex meshes, often using spatial partitioning to optimize the process.


the narrow phase is more computationally expensive than the broad phase, but it is necessary for accurate collision detection and response.





contact manifold

a contact manifold is a data structure that describes the points of contact between two colliding objects, including:

1. contact points - where the objects are touching

2. contact normal - a vector that points from one object to the other

3. penetration depth - how much the objects are overlapping

it is used to calculate the response to a collision, such as applying forces to separate the objects and simulate realistic interactions.





constraint solvers

constraint solvers are used to handle constraints between objects, such as joints, ropes, or hinges.

they work by calculating the forces needed to maintain the constraints and applying them to the objects involved.

a simple example of a constraint solver is a spring constraint, which applies a force proportional to the distance between two objects to keep them at a certain distance from each other.


Iterative solver: a common approach to solving constraints is to use an iterative solver, which repeatedly applies corrections to the positions and velocities of the objects for a fixed number of iterations.





resolving collisions

after detecting a collision and calculating the contact manifold, we need to resolve the collision by applying forces to the objects to separate them and simulate realistic interactions.

this typically involves calculating the impulse needed to change the velocities of the objects based on their masses, velocities, and the contact normal.

the impulse can be calculated using the following formula:

J = -(1 + e) * (v_rel · n) / (1/m1 + 1/m2)

where J is the impulse, e is the coefficient of restitution (which determines how bouncy the collision is), v_rel is the relative velocity of the objects at the contact point, n is the contact normal, and m1 and m2 are the masses of the objects.






a frictional impulse can also be calculated to simulate the effects of friction between the objects:

J_friction = -μ * J * t

where J_friction is the frictional impulse, μ is the coefficient of friction, J is the normal impulse calculated earlier, and t is the tangent vector at the contact point.


once the impulse is calculated, we can apply it to the velocities of the objects to simulate the collision response.





challenges in physics simulation

1. stability: ensuring that the simulation remains stable and does not produce unrealistic results, especially when using larger time steps.

2. accuracy: balancing the need for accurate simulations with the computational cost, as more accurate methods can be more expensive to compute.

3. collision handling: dealing with complex collision scenarios, such as stacking objects or simulating soft body interactions.

4. performance: optimizing the simulation to run efficiently in real-time applications, such as video games or virtual reality.





stability vs accuracy

there is often a trade-off between stability and accuracy in physics simulation. More accurate methods can be computationally expensive and may require smaller time steps to remain stable, while less accurate methods can be faster but may produce unrealistic results.

for example, the Euler method is simple and fast but can be unstable for larger time steps, while more advanced methods like Runge-Kutta or Verlet integration can provide better accuracy and stability at the cost of increased computational complexity.

Jitter & the stacking problem: when simulating a stack of objects, small errors in the simulation can accumulate over time, causing the stack to become unstable and collapse. This is often due to the way collisions are resolved and can be mitigated by using more accurate collision response methods or by implementing additional constraints to keep the objects in place.






performance optimization

optimizing the performance of a physics engine is crucial for real-time applications, such as video games or virtual reality. Some common techniques for improving performance include:

1. Spatial Partitioning: using spatial data structures (e.g., grids, quadtrees, octrees) to efficiently query for nearby objects and reduce the number of collision checks.

2. Level of Detail (LOD): using simpler physics simulations for distant or less important objects to reduce computational load.

3. Multithreading: leveraging multiple CPU cores to parallelize the simulation and improve performance.

4. GPU Acceleration: using the GPU to perform physics calculations in parallel, which can significantly improve performance for complex simulations.





production-grade engines

to implement a production-grade physics engine, we would need to consider additional features and optimizations, such as:

1. Support for more complex shapes: implementing support for a wider variety of shapes, such as concave meshes or soft bodies.

2. Advanced collision response: implementing more sophisticated collision response methods to handle complex interactions between objects.

3. Advanced constraints: implementing more complex constraints, such as ragdoll physics or vehicle simulations.

4. Multithreading and GPU acceleration: optimizing the engine to take advantage of modern hardware capabilities for improved performance.

5. Integration with rendering engines: ensuring that the physics engine can be easily integrated with popular rendering engines (e.g., Unity, Unreal Engine) for use in games and other applications.

6. Robustness and stability: implementing additional features to improve the stability and robustness of the engine, such as better handling of edge cases and more accurate numerical methods.

physics engines play a crucial role in creating immersive and realistic experiences in video games, virtual reality, scientific simulations, and robotics. By understanding the underlying principles and techniques of physics simulation, developers can create more engaging and interactive applications.
